This post is mainly a re-formulation and detailed expansion for the section 8.4, "Direct Method" in "14 lectures on Visual SLAM".

When I was working on direct method SLAM, I found the notations used on the book is hard to understand and many details to derive the final equation are omitted. Hence, I wrote this post as a note and record for my own derivation of the Jacobian in Direct Method of SLAM.

Coordinate System and Symbol Table

Assumption

Cam1 and Cam2 have same intrinsic matrix KKK and are pinhole camera with no distortion (or distortion is corrected for resulting image in pre-process step).

Coordinate System

There are two 3D coordinate systems: the camera 1 (where optic center O1O_1O1​ is the same as world coordinate origin) and the camera 2 (with transformation of cam1 → cam2 is T12=ξT_1^2 = \xiT12​=ξ)

There are two 2D coordinate systems (pixel coordinates): the image 1 and image 2. I will use uv coordinate when refer to them.

Step 1. Reprojection Model

According to the pinhole camera model, we have p1,i~=KPi\tilde{p_{1, i}} = KP_ip1,i​~​=KPi​ and p2,i~=K(Exp(ξ)Pi~)1:3\tilde{p_{2, i}} = K(Exp(\xi)\tilde{P_i})_{1:3}p2,i​~​=K(Exp(ξ)Pi​~​)1:3​.

By converting homogenous coordinate to heterogeneous coordinate, we can retrieve the p1,ip_{1, i}p1,i​ and p2,ip_{2, i}p2,i​ from ξ\xiξ and KKK.

Step 2. Photometric Error as Model Residual

In direct method, we do not do feature point matching as this process (extracting feature point, convert to feature descriptor, run descriptor matching with or without epipolar geo constraint) is computationally heavy and error-prone.

Instead, we use the "illuminance consistency assumption". That is, between two camera frames ttt and t+1t + 1t+1, the illuminance of a point in 3D space should be consistent. That is,

It(P)=It+1(P)I_t(P) = I_{t + 1}(P)It​(P)=It+1​(P)

Naturally, the photometric error is the difference in illuminance between same point in 2 adjacent frames.

ei(ξ)=(I1(p1,i)−I2(p2,i))2e_i(\xi) = \left(I_1(p_{1, i}) - I_2(p_{2, i})\right)^2ei​(ξ)=(I1​(p1,i​)−I2​(p2,i​))2

Direct SLAM make use of this assumption heavily. In the bundle adjustment process, we define the total residual of current state estimation as:

R(ξ)=∑i=1Nei(ξ)=∑i=1N(I1(p1,i)−I2(p2,i))2\mathbf{R}(\xi) = \sum_{i = 1}^N{e_i(\xi)} = \sum_{i = 1}^N{\left(I_1(p_{1, i}) - I_2(p_{2, i})\right)^2}R(ξ)=i=1∑N​ei​(ξ)=i=1∑N​(I1​(p1,i​)−I2​(p2,i​))2

Step 3. Optimization Problem Formulation

During the (local) bundle adjustment stage, we want to optimize the pose of cam2, namely ξ∈se(3)\xi \in \mathfrak{se}(3)ξ∈se(3), to minimize the residual of state estimation. The mathematical formulation of this optimization problem is:

ξ⋆=arg⁡min⁡ξR(ξ)=arg⁡min⁡ξ∑i=1Nei(ξ)=arg⁡min⁡ξ∑i=1N∥I1(p1,i)−I2(p2,i)∥22=arg⁡min⁡ξ∑i=1N∥I1(KPi)−I2(K(Exp(ξ)Pi~)1:3∥22\begin{aligned} \xi^\star &= \arg\min_{\xi}{\mathbf{R}(\xi)}\\ &= \arg\min_{\xi}{\sum_{i = 1}^N{e_i(\xi)}}\\ &= \arg\min_{\xi}{\sum_{i = 1}^N{\|I_1(p_{1, i}) - I_2(p_{2, i})\|_2^2}}\\ &= \arg\min_{\xi}{\sum_{i = 1}^N{\|I_1(KP_i) - I_2(K(Exp(\xi)\tilde{P_i})_{1:3}\|_2^2}} \end{aligned}ξ⋆​=argξmin​R(ξ)=argξmin​i=1∑N​ei​(ξ)=argξmin​i=1∑N​∥I1​(p1,i​)−I2​(p2,i​)∥22​=argξmin​i=1∑N​∥I1​(KPi​)−I2​(K(Exp(ξ)Pi​~​)1:3​∥22​​

Step 4. Perturbation Model and Jacobian for Direct Method

To solve this optimization problem, we can use optimizers like Gauss-Newton or Lagrange-Marquadt. These optimizers typically requires us to provide the jacobian of model. Hence, we need to derive the jacobian J\mathbf{J}J for cam2 pose ξ\xiξ w.r.t. R\mathbf{R}R. Since R\mathbf{R}R is a simple summation from ei(ξ)e_i(\xi)ei​(ξ), we will only derive ∂ei(ξ)/∂ξ\partial e_i(\xi) / \partial \xi∂ei​(ξ)/∂ξ here.

To calculate the jacobian of R\mathbf{R}R w.r.t. ξ\xiξ, there are two main methods: the direct differentiation and perturbation model.

Direct Differentiation (which, we will not use)

∂ei(ξ)∂ξ=lim⁡δξ→0ei(ξ⊕δξ)−ei(ξ)δξ=lim⁡δξ→0∥I(KPi)−I2(K(Exp(ξ⊕δξ)Pi~)1:3)∥22−ei(ξ)δξ\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial e_i(\xi)}{\partial \xi} &= \lim_{\delta\xi \to 0}\frac{e_i(\xi \oplus \delta\xi) - e_i(\xi)}{\delta\xi}\\ &= \lim_{\delta\xi\to 0}{\frac{\|I(KP_i) - I_2(K(Exp(\xi\oplus\delta\xi)\tilde{P_i})_{1:3})\|_2^2 - e_i(\xi)}{\delta\xi}} \end{aligned}∂ξ∂ei​(ξ)​​=δξ→0lim​δξei​(ξ⊕δξ)−ei​(ξ)​=δξ→0lim​δξ∥I(KPi​)−I2​(K(Exp(ξ⊕δξ)Pi​~​)1:3​)∥22​−ei​(ξ)​​

However, we have ξ⊕δξ≠Log(Exp(ξ)Exp(δξ))\xi\oplus\delta\xi \neq Log(Exp(\xi)Exp(\delta\xi))ξ⊕δξ=Log(Exp(ξ)Exp(δξ)) in Lie algebra. Instead, we need to follow the BCH formula with first-order approximation (when δξ\delta\xiδξ is sufficiently small)

Log(Exp(δξ)Exp(ξ))≈Jl(ξ)−1δξ+ξLog(Exp(\delta\xi)Exp(\xi)) \approx \mathbf{J}_l(\xi)^{-1}\delta\xi + \xiLog(Exp(δξ)Exp(ξ))≈Jl​(ξ)−1δξ+ξ

However, using BCH formula requires us to calculate the Jl\mathbf{J}_lJl​, which is undesired due to its computation complexity. Hence, we will use the perturbation model to calculate the differentiation instead.

Perturbation Model

Comparing to the direct differentiation, where BCH formula is required, perturbation model first add a small perturbation on target function eie_iei​, then calculate the derivative of residual w.r.t. the perturbation term.

That is, we will calculate ∂ei(ξ⊕δξ)∂δξ\frac{\partial e_i(\xi \oplus \delta\xi)}{\partial \delta\xi}∂δξ∂ei​(ξ⊕δξ)​ instead of ∂ei(ξ)∂ξ\frac{\partial e_i(\xi)}{\partial \xi}∂ξ∂ei​(ξ)​.

First, we will derive ei(ξ⊕δξ)e_i(\xi \oplus \delta\xi)ei​(ξ⊕δξ) (using left-perturbation, the result is different from right-perturbation).

ei(ξ⊕δξ)=I1(KPi)−I2(K(Exp(δξ)Exp(ξ)Pi~)1:3)\begin{aligned} e_i(\xi\oplus\delta\xi) &= I_1(KP_i) - I_2(K(Exp(\delta\xi)Exp(\xi)\tilde{P_i})_{1:3})\\ \end{aligned}ei​(ξ⊕δξ)​=I1​(KPi​)−I2​(K(Exp(δξ)Exp(ξ)Pi​~​)1:3​)​

The taylor expansion for Exp(δξ)Exp(\delta \xi)Exp(δξ) is of form

Exp(δξ)=exp(δξ∧)=∑n=0∞1n!(δξ∧)nExp(\delta\xi) = exp(\delta\xi^\wedge) = \sum_{n = 0}^\infty{\frac{1}{n!}(\delta\xi^\wedge)^n}Exp(δξ)=exp(δξ∧)=n=0∑∞​n!1​(δξ∧)n

Using a first-order taylor approximation here (should be accurate as δξ\delta\xiδξ is a minute perturbation term), we have

Exp(δξ)≈1+δξ∧Exp(\delta\xi) \approx 1 + \delta\xi^\wedgeExp(δξ)≈1+δξ∧

Then, we have

ei(ξ⊕δξ)=I1(KPi)−I2(K(Exp(δξ)Exp(ξ)P~i)1:3)≈I1(KPi)−I2(K((1+δξ∧)Exp(ξ)P~i)1:3)=I1(KPi)−I2(K(Exp(ξ)P~i)1:3+K(δξ∧Exp(ξ)P~i)1:3)\begin{aligned} e_i(\xi\oplus\delta\xi) &= I_1(KP_i) - I_2(K(Exp(\delta\xi)Exp(\xi)\tilde{P}_i)_{1:3})\\ &\approx I_1(KP_i) - I_2(K((1 + \delta\xi^\wedge)Exp(\xi)\tilde{P}_i)_{1:3}) \\ &= I_1(KP_i) - I_2(K(Exp(\xi)\tilde{P}_i)_{1:3} + K(\delta\xi^\wedge Exp(\xi)\tilde{P}_i)_{1:3}) \end{aligned}ei​(ξ⊕δξ)​=I1​(KPi​)−I2​(K(Exp(δξ)Exp(ξ)P~i​)1:3​)≈I1​(KPi​)−I2​(K((1+δξ∧)Exp(ξ)P~i​)1:3​)=I1​(KPi​)−I2​(K(Exp(ξ)P~i​)1:3​+K(δξ∧Exp(ξ)P~i​)1:3​)​

Then we apply another first-order taylor approximation

≈I2(K(Exp(ξ)P~i)1:3+K(δξ∧Exp(ξ)P~i)1:3)≈I2(K(Exp(ξ)P~i)1:3)+∂I2(p2)∂p2∂p2∂ξδξ\begin{aligned} &\phantom{\approx}I_2(K(Exp(\xi)\tilde{P}_i)_{1:3} + K(\delta\xi^\wedge Exp(\xi)\tilde{P}_i)_{1:3}) \\ &\approx I_2(K(Exp(\xi)\tilde{P}_i)_{1:3}) + \frac{\partial I_2(p_2)}{\partial p_2} \frac{\partial p_2}{\partial \xi} \delta\xi \end{aligned}​≈I2​(K(Exp(ξ)P~i​)1:3​+K(δξ∧Exp(ξ)P~i​)1:3​)≈I2​(K(Exp(ξ)P~i​)1:3​)+∂p2​∂I2​(p2​)​∂ξ∂p2​​δξ​

And we have the fully expanded form of e(ξ⊕δξ)e(\xi\oplus\delta\xi)e(ξ⊕δξ)

e(ξ⊕δξ)=I1(KPi)−(I2(K(Exp(δξ)Exp(ξ)Pi)1:3)+∂I2(p2)∂p2∂p2∂ξδξ)e(\xi \oplus \delta\xi) = I_1(KP_i) - \left(I_2(K(Exp(\delta\xi)Exp(\xi)P_i)_{1:3}) + \frac{\partial I_2(p_2)}{\partial p_2} \frac{\partial p_2}{\partial \xi} \delta\xi \right)e(ξ⊕δξ)=I1​(KPi​)−(I2​(K(Exp(δξ)Exp(ξ)Pi​)1:3​)+∂p2​∂I2​(p2​)​∂ξ∂p2​​δξ)

where we can derive the derivative under left perturbation

∂e(ξ⊕δξ)∂δξ≈−∂I2(p2)∂p2∂p2∂ξ\frac{\partial e(\xi \oplus \delta\xi)}{\partial \delta\xi} \approx -\frac{\partial I_2(p_2)}{\partial p_2}\frac{\partial p_2}{\partial \xi}∂δξ∂e(ξ⊕δξ)​≈−∂p2​∂I2​(p2​)​∂ξ∂p2​​
